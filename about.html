About this artifact.

De Formulieren is the second annual report of a Dutch government authority that does not yet exist. The institution recognises and pays for the invisible labour that currently keeps Dutch precision-agriculture running.

What is real, what is imagined.

The artifact is built around a single real interview with a senior farm advisor at a regional cooperative in the Eastern Netherlands, conducted on 16 April 2026. The labour the report counts — articulation work, repair, knowledge translation, care — is old and well documented. The legal authority that recognises this labour, Rijksdienst voor Gedeeld Beheer, is invented for the project.

The killer quote on every page is verbatim:

Field note · Deventer · 16 April 2026

"My farmers are not afraid of apps. They are afraid of the forms the app is connected to."

The Foqus Planet sensors, the Beheerderskeurmerk score, the cow #17 incident, the WhatsApp groups titled "what to answer when the inspector asks…" — these are condensed from interview material. The names, codes, and form numbers are invented to give the artifact the texture of a real document.

Why this form.

Speculative design (Dunne & Raby, Auger, Superflux) builds objects from a possible future to argue about the present. The annual report of a fictional authority makes a future visible enough that the present can be discussed without slipping into prediction or polemic.

The two-act story you can play — barn, then form — is the smallest version of the argument the report makes at length: that the cost of legibility is borne by the people the platform doesn't see, and that there exists a kind of institution that could count this cost.

What I used AI for.

Two things, both declared:

  • The coat of arms is a vector generated by Recraft V4.1 from a written brief describing what the symbols should mean (shield, wheat sheaf, sensor band, three rules of the cooperative).
  • The eight atmospheric illustrations in the story-game are generated by FLUX.1 [dev] via fal.ai from prompts describing each scene in Rembrandt-style oil-paint, navy-and-cream palette.

All the writing — the booklet, the form prompts, the story choices, the killer quote framing, the case files — is mine, except the verbatim interview quote above and the academic citations.

Things I would do differently.

One interview is not a sample. A real version of this report would interview the farmers themselves, not just their advisor; would have a Dutch translation; would be reviewed by the cooperative before publication. The current artifact is a first sketch from a single voice — declared as such, and stronger for the declaration.
